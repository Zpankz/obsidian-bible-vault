# ἀγάπη — Agape

The distinctively Christian term for love, describing the self-giving, unconditional love that originates in God and is poured out through Christ. While not absent from pre-Christian Greek, the NT authors transformed this word into the supreme vocabulary of divine affection.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| God's love for humanity | Divine, salvific love | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/John/John 3#v16\|John 3:16]] |
| Love as God's nature | Ontological attribute | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/1 John/1 John 4#v8\|1 John 4:8]] |
| Christian ethical demand | Self-sacrificial love toward others | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 13#v4\|1 Cor 13:4-7]] |
| The love feast | Communal meal of the church | Jude 12 |

## Theological Significance

The NT deliberately favors ἀγάπη over ἔρως (eros, absent from the NT) and φιλία (philia, "friendship"). Agape is not a feeling but an act of will — God *decides* to love; believers are *commanded* to love (John 13:34). This is why Paul can describe love as a series of actions ("love is patient, love is kind") rather than emotions.

The theological climax is 1 John 4:8 — "God **is** agape" — making love not merely something God does but what God *is*.

## OT Background

The Hebrew [[concepts/hebrew/H157 - ahav|אָהַב]] (*ahav*) covers the full range of love — God's covenantal love for Israel (Deut 7:8), the command to love God (Deut 6:5), and love of neighbor (Lev 19:18). The LXX chose ἀγαπάω/ἀγάπη to translate these, giving the Greek term its theological depth.
