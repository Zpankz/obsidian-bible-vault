# ἐκκλησία — Ekklesia

Literally "the called-out assembly" — those summoned from the wider populace for a public gathering. In secular Greek, it denoted the citizen assembly of a city-state. The NT adopts this term for the community of believers gathered around Christ.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Universal church | The whole body of believers | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Ephesians/Ephesians 1#v22\|Eph 1:22-23]] |
| Local congregation | A specific gathered community | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/1 Corinthians/1 Corinthians 1#v2\|1 Cor 1:2]] |
| House church | Believers meeting in a home | Rom 16:5; Col 4:15 |
| Secular assembly | Public civic gathering | Acts 19:39 |

## Theological Significance

Paul's ecclesiology centers on the body metaphor (1 Cor 12; Eph 4): the ekklesia is the "body of Christ," with Christ as head (Col 1:18). This is more than analogy — the church is the continuing physical presence of Christ in the world.

Ephesians 5:25-27 presents the church as the bride of Christ, for whom He gave Himself up "so that He might sanctify her." The church's identity is thus christological at its core — it exists only in relation to Christ.

## OT Background

The LXX uses ἐκκλησία to translate the Hebrew קָהָל (*qahal*) — the assembled congregation of Israel, especially at Sinai (Deut 9:10) and in worship (Ps 22:22). Stephen calls Israel in the wilderness "the ekklesia in the desert" (Acts 7:38), explicitly connecting the NT church to OT Israel as God's covenant assembly.
