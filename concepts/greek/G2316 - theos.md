# θεός — Theos

The standard Greek word for God, used 1,317 times in the NT. It translates the Hebrew Elohim and becomes the primary designation for the God of Israel and the Father of Jesus Christ. Significantly, it is also applied to Jesus (John 1:1; 20:28; Rom 9:5).

## Theological Significance

The NT inherits OT monotheism but redefines it christologically and trinitarianly. Paul's reformulated Shema (1 Cor 8:6) — "one God, the Father… and one Lord, Jesus Christ" — includes Jesus within the identity of the one God of Israel without abandoning monotheism.

John 1:1 makes the most direct claim: "the Word was God" (θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος). Thomas's confession — "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28) — is the climactic christological statement of the Fourth Gospel.

## OT Background

The LXX renders [[concepts/hebrew/H430 - Elohim|אֱלֹהִים]] (*Elohim*) as θεός throughout. The Hebrew plural-with-singular-verbs becomes a straightforward Greek singular — but the NT re-introduces complexity by applying θεός to both the Father and the Son, creating the theological space for trinitarian confession.
