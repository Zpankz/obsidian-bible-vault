# χάρις — Charis

Grace — unmerited divine favor, the free gift of God. In Paul, it becomes the defining concept of the gospel: salvation is "by grace through faith… not of works" (Eph 2:8-9). The English word "charity" derives from charis through Latin *caritas*, though the semantic range is broader.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| God's saving grace | Unmerited favor toward sinners | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Ephesians/Ephesians 2#v8\|Eph 2:8]] |

| Thankfulness | Gratitude (the response to grace) | 1 Cor 15:57; Col 3:16 |

## Theological Significance

Grace in Paul is not a divine substance infused into the soul but God's free, active disposition toward the undeserving. "Where sin increased, grace abounded all the more" (Rom 5:20) — grace is always greater than sin because it originates in God's character, not human merit.

John 1:14-17 sets up the key contrast: "The law was given through Moses; grace and truth were realized through Jesus Christ." Grace is not the negation of law but its fulfillment — what the law demanded, grace provides.

## OT Background

The Hebrew חֵן (*chen*) means favor, graciousness — "Noah found favor in the eyes of the LORD" (Gen 6:8). The related חֶסֶד ([[concepts/hebrew/H2617 - chesed|chesed]], "loyal love/steadfast love") provides the covenantal dimension of grace: God's grace is not arbitrary kindness but covenant faithfulness to an undeserving people.
