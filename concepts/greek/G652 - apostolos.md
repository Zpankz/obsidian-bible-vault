# ἀπόστολος — Apostolos

One who is sent with the authority of the sender — a commissioned representative. In the NT, the term designates primarily the Twelve chosen by Christ and Paul, but also extends to other commissioned missionaries (Rom 16:7; 2 Cor 8:23).

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| The Twelve | Christ's chosen witnesses | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Luke/Luke 6#v13\|Luke 6:13]] |

| Christ Himself | The sent one of the Father | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Hebrews/Hebrews 3#v1\|Heb 3:1]] |
| Broader missionaries | Church delegates | 2 Cor 8:23; Phil 2:25 |

## Theological Significance

The apostle is not self-appointed but divinely commissioned. The authority of an apostle derives entirely from the one who sends — this is why Paul insists his apostleship is "not from men nor through the agency of man, but through Jesus Christ and God the Father" (Gal 1:1). The apostolic witness to the resurrection is foundational to the church (Eph 2:20).

## OT Background

The Hebrew concept of *shaliach* (authorized agent) provides the legal background: "a man's agent is as himself." When God "sends" (שָׁלַח, *shalach*) Moses (Exod 3:10) or the prophets (Isa 6:8), they speak with divine authority. The apostle inherits this prophetic commission.
