# θάνατος — Thanatos

Death — the wages of sin (Rom 6:23) and the "last enemy" to be abolished (1 Cor 15:26). The NT treats death not as natural but as an intruder — the consequence of sin that Christ defeats through His own death and resurrection.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Physical death | Cessation of bodily life | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Romans/Romans 5#v12\|Rom 5:12]] |
| Spiritual death | Separation from God | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Ephesians/Ephesians 2#v1\|Eph 2:1]] |
| Christ's death | His atoning, substitutionary death | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Philippians/Philippians 2#v8\|Phil 2:8]] |
| Second death | Eschatological judgment | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Revelation/Revelation 20#v14\|Rev 20:14]] |

## Theological Significance

Paul personifies death as a cosmic tyrant: "death reigned from Adam to Moses" (Rom 5:14). Death entered through sin (Rom 5:12) and reigns as sin's paymaster (Rom 6:23). But Christ's resurrection breaks death's dominion — "death no longer is master over Him" (Rom 6:9), and through Him, "death is swallowed up in victory" (1 Cor 15:54).

Revelation 20:14 depicts the ultimate defeat: "Death and Hades were thrown into the lake of fire. This is the second death." Death itself dies.

## OT Background

Death enters the biblical narrative as divine warning (Gen 2:17) and consequence (Gen 3:19). The Hebrew מָוֶת (*maveth*) is sometimes personified as a power (Isa 25:8 — "He will swallow up death for all time"; Hos 13:14 — "O death, where are your thorns?"). Paul quotes both texts in his resurrection argument (1 Cor 15:54-55).
