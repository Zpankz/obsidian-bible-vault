# πατήρ — Pater

Father — Jesus' distinctive name for God, used over 170 times in the Gospels. While the OT occasionally calls God "Father" (Isa 63:16; 64:8), Jesus makes it His primary mode of address, and His use of the Aramaic *Abba* (Mark 14:36) expresses unprecedented intimacy. Through Christ, believers are adopted and may also call God "Father" (Rom 8:15).

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Matthew/Matthew 6#v9|Matt 6:9]] — "Our Father (*pater*) who is in heaven"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/John/John 5#v19|John 5:19]] — "The Son can do nothing of Himself unless… He sees the Father (*pater*) doing"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/John/John 14#v9|John 14:9]] — "He who has seen Me has seen the Father (*pater*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Romans/Romans 8#v15|Rom 8:15]] — "You have received a spirit of adoption… by which we cry out, 'Abba! Father (*pater*)!'"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Ephesians/Ephesians 1#v3|Eph 1:3]] — "The God and Father (*pater*) of our Lord Jesus Christ"

## Theological Significance

Calling God *Pater* is not a metaphor Jesus chose among alternatives — it reveals the eternal relationship within the Godhead. The Father is Father because He eternally has a Son. Trinitarian theology begins here: the names "Father" and "Son" are not imposed titles but relational identities within the divine being.

Through union with Christ, believers participate in the Son's relationship to the Father. "Abba, Father" (Gal 4:6; Rom 8:15) is the Spirit's cry within the believer — the adopted child shares the natural Son's intimacy. The Lord's Prayer's opening — "Our Father" — is revolutionary: the God of cosmic majesty can be addressed as a child addresses a parent.
