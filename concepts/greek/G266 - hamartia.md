# ἁμαρτία — Hamartia

The primary NT word for sin, derived from an archery term meaning "to miss the mark." Paul elevates it beyond individual acts into a cosmic power — Sin personified as a tyrant that enslaves humanity (Rom 5-8).

| Context | Meaning | Example |

| Cosmic power | Sin as ruling force | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Romans/Romans 6#v12\|Rom 6:12]] |

| Human condition | Universal fallenness | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Romans/Romans 3#v23\|Rom 3:23]] |

## Theological Significance

Paul's distinctive contribution is treating ἁμαρτία as a personified power. In Romans 5-8, Sin "entered the world" (5:12), "reigned in death" (5:21), is a master who pays "wages" (6:23), and "dwells in" the believer (7:17). This is not merely moral failure but an enslaving dominion from which only Christ's death can liberate.

The decisive theological statement is Romans 3:23 — "all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God" — establishing universal human need as the premise for universal grace.

## OT Background

The Hebrew [[concepts/hebrew/H2403 - chattath|חַטָּאת]] (*chattath*) also means both "sin" and "sin offering" — the same word names the disease and the cure. This dual meaning is echoed in 2 Cor 5:21, where Christ is made ἁμαρτία ("sin" or "sin offering") on our behalf.
