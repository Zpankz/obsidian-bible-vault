# ἄγγελος — Angelos

The Greek word for "messenger," used both of human envoys and of heavenly beings who serve as God's agents. The English word "angel" is a direct transliteration rather than a translation — obscuring the fact that the word fundamentally means one who is sent with a message.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| Heavenly beings | Angels in God's service | [[bibles/NASB-IL/Hebrews/Hebrews 1#v14\|Heb 1:14]] |
| The Angel of the Lord | Theophanic messenger | Matt 1:20 |
| Human messengers | John's disciples | Luke 7:24 |
| Church leaders | "Angels" of the seven churches | Rev 2:1 |

## Theological Significance

The NT consistently subordinates angelic beings to Christ (Heb 1:4-14). Angels are "ministering spirits" (Heb 1:14), not objects of worship (Col 2:18; Rev 22:8-9). They announce pivotal moments — the incarnation (Luke 1:26-38), the resurrection (Matt 28:2-7), and the ascension (Acts 1:10-11).

## OT Background

The Hebrew [[concepts/hebrew/H4397 - malak|מַלְאָךְ]] (*malak*) carries identical double meaning — human messenger (Gen 32:3) and divine agent (Gen 16:7). The "Angel of YHWH" (*malak YHWH*) is often identified with YHWH Himself, a figure the NT implicitly connects to the pre-incarnate Christ.
