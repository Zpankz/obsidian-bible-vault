# ἅγιος — Hagios

The primary NT adjective for holiness, describing what is set apart for God and partaking of His nature. It modifies the Spirit (τὸ πνεῦμα τὸ ἅγιον), the people of God ("saints"), and God Himself.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| The Holy Spirit | Third person of the Trinity | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Acts/Acts 2#v4\|Acts 2:4]] |
| Believers as "saints" | The set-apart community | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Romans/Romans 1#v7\|Rom 1:7]] |
| God's holiness | Divine otherness | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Revelation/Revelation 4#v8\|Rev 4:8]] |
| Sacred places/things | Temple, Scriptures | Matt 24:15; Rom 1:2 |

## Theological Significance

Holiness in the NT is both positional (believers *are* saints by calling, 1 Cor 1:2) and progressive (they are to *become* holy, 1 Pet 1:15-16). The call "Be holy, for I am holy" (quoting Lev 11:44) bridges the testaments — the same God who set Israel apart now sets apart the church.

The triple "Holy, holy, holy" (Rev 4:8, echoing Isa 6:3) is the only divine attribute repeated three times in Scripture — holiness is the attribute that qualifies all others.

## OT Background

The Hebrew [[concepts/hebrew/H6944 - qodesh|קֹדֶשׁ]] (*qodesh*) denotes radical separation — the "otherness" of God. What God touches becomes holy; what is holy must not be profaned. The LXX consistently renders *qodesh* with ἅγιος, carrying the entire OT holiness theology into the NT.
