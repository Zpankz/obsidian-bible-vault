# εὐαγγέλιον — Euangelion

Good news — originally a secular term for a report of military victory or a royal proclamation. In the NT, it becomes the technical term for the message about Jesus Christ: His life, death, resurrection, and lordship. The gospel is not advice but announcement — not what we must do but what God has done.

## Theological Significance

Paul identifies the gospel as "the power of God for salvation" (Rom 1:16) — the euangelion is not merely *about* power but *is* power. In 1 Corinthians 15:1-8, Paul defines the gospel's content: "Christ died for our sins… was buried… was raised on the third day." This is the irreducible core.

The gospel is also a present tense reality — Paul "gospels" (εὐαγγελίζω) as an ongoing activity (1 Cor 9:16), and the gospel continues to "bear fruit and increase" (Col 1:6) in the world.

## OT Background

Isaiah 40:9 and 52:7 proclaim good tidings (בָּשַׂר, *basar*): "How lovely on the mountains are the feet of him who brings good news." The LXX renders this with εὐαγγελίζω. Jesus reads Isaiah 61:1 in the Nazareth synagogue (Luke 4:18) — "to preach good news to the poor" — and declares it fulfilled in Himself.
