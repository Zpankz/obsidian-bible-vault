# δικαιοσύνη — Dikaiosyne

Righteousness — the state of being in right standing before God. In Paul, it becomes a forensic term: God declares the ungodly righteous on the basis of faith (Rom 4:5), not on the basis of law-keeping. This is the "righteousness of God" — both God's own righteous character and the righteous status He grants.

## Semantic Range

| Context | Meaning | Example |
|:---|:---|:---|
| God's righteousness | His faithful, saving justice | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Romans/Romans 1#v17\|Rom 1:17]] |
| Imputed righteousness | Credited through faith | [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Romans/Romans 4#v5\|Rom 4:5]] |
| Ethical conduct | Right behavior | Matt 5:6, 20; 6:1 |
| Eschatological justice | God's final verdict | 2 Tim 4:8 |

## Theological Significance

Romans 1:17 — "the righteousness of God is revealed from faith to faith" — is the thesis of Paul's greatest letter. This δικαιοσύνη is simultaneously God's attribute (He *is* righteous) and His gift (He *gives* righteousness to those who believe). The cross solves the paradox of Romans 3:26 — God is "just and the justifier" of the one who has faith in Jesus.

In Matthew, righteousness has a more ethical orientation: "Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness" (Matt 5:6).

## OT Background

The Hebrew צְדָקָה (*tsedaqah*) and its cognates describe God's covenantal faithfulness — He acts "righteously" when He keeps His promises and delivers His people (Isa 45:8; 51:5-6). Paul inherits this salvation-as-righteousness tradition, making the cross the definitive act of God's covenant faithfulness.
