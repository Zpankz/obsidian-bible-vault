# μετανοέω — Metanoeo

To change one's mind and direction — a fundamental reorientation of the whole person toward God. Though the Greek etymology suggests a change of thinking (*meta* + *nous*), the NT usage, shaped by the Hebrew *shuv* ("to turn/return"), involves the entire person: mind, will, and behavior.

## Theological Significance

Repentance is the human side of the gospel encounter — the turning that corresponds to God's grace. It is the first word of Jesus' public ministry: "Repent, for the kingdom of heaven is at hand" (Matt 4:17). Peter's Pentecost sermon makes it the proper response to the proclamation of Christ: "Repent, and each of you be baptized" (Acts 2:38).

Importantly, repentance is itself a gift of God (Acts 5:31; 11:18; 2 Tim 2:25) — the human turn is enabled by divine initiative.

## OT Background

The Hebrew שׁוּב (*shuv*, "to turn, return") is the OT's primary repentance vocabulary — it means physically turning around and walking back to God. The prophets plead for Israel to "return" (Jer 3:12; Hos 14:1; Joel 2:12). The NT μετανοέω inherits this directional force: repentance is not mere regret but a U-turn of the whole life.
