# יוֹם — Yom

Day — used for a literal day (24-hour period or daylight portion), a general period of time, or an eschatological epoch ("the day of the LORD"). The creation narrative structures reality around *yom*: "there was evening and there was morning, one day." Time itself is a created, ordered reality.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v5|Gen 1:5]] — "God called the light day (*yom*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 2#v4|Gen 2:4]] — "In the day (*yom*) that the LORD God made earth and heaven"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Joel/Joel 2#v1|Joel 2:1]] — "The day (*yom*) of the LORD is coming"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Amos/Amos 5#v18|Amos 5:18]] — "Alas, you who are longing for the day (*yom*) of the LORD"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 90#v4|Ps 90:4]] — "A thousand years in Your sight are like yesterday (*yom*)"

## Theological Significance

The seven-day structure of Genesis 1 establishes the week as the basic rhythm of human life, culminating in Sabbath rest. Whether *yom* in Genesis 1 denotes a literal 24-hour day or a figurative period has been debated throughout church history, but the theological point is clear: time is God's creation, ordered and purposeful.

The prophetic concept of the "Day of the LORD" (*yom YHWH*) transforms *yom* into an eschatological term — a coming epoch of divine judgment and vindication. Amos shockingly reverses Israel's expectation: the Day of the LORD will be darkness, not light, for a disobedient people. This concept flows into the NT's "day of the Lord" (1 Thess 5:2; 2 Pet 3:10).
