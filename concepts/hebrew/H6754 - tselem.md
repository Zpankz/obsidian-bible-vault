# צֶלֶם — Tselem

Image — the word used in Genesis 1:26-27 to describe humanity's unique relationship to God: "Let Us make man in Our image (*tselem*)." In the ancient Near East, a *tselem* was a royal statue placed in a territory to represent the king's authority. Humanity is God's living image in creation — His authorized representative.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v26|Gen 1:26]] — "Let Us make man in Our image (*tselem*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v27|Gen 1:27]] — "God created man in His own image (*tselem*)"

## Theological Significance

The *imago Dei* is the foundational anthropological claim of Scripture: every human being bears God's image, conferring infinite dignity and grounding all human rights (Gen 9:6). The image is not a part of the person (like reason or soul) but a function — humanity represents God's rule in creation, exercising [[concepts/hebrew/H7287 - radah|dominion]] as God's vice-regents.

The NT reveals that Christ is the perfect *tselem* — "the image (*eikon*) of the invisible God" (Col 1:15). Fallen humanity bears a marred image; salvation is the restoration of the image through conformity to Christ (Rom 8:29; 2 Cor 3:18). The trajectory runs from creation (Gen 1:26) through fall through redemption to glorification — the image damaged, restored, and perfected.
