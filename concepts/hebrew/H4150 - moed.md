# מוֹעֵד — Moed

Appointed time, designated meeting, or festival — from the root *yaad* ("to appoint, designate"). *Moed* refers both to the appointed festivals of Israel (Passover, Tabernacles, etc.) and to the Tent of Meeting (*ohel moed*), the place where God and Israel rendezvous. God structures time and space for encounter.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v14|Gen 1:14]] — "Let them be for signs and for seasons (*moedim*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Leviticus/Leviticus 23#v2|Lev 23:2]] — "The appointed times (*moedim*) of the LORD"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Exodus/Exodus 33#v7|Exod 33:7]] — "The tent of meeting (*ohel moed*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Daniel/Daniel 12#v7|Dan 12:7]] — "A time (*moed*), times, and half a time"

## Theological Significance

*Moed* reveals that biblical time is not uniform but punctuated — God builds meeting points into the calendar. The festivals are not human inventions but divine appointments: "the *moedim* of the LORD which you shall proclaim" (Lev 23:2). Sacred time and sacred space converge at the *moed*.

The celestial bodies themselves were created to mark *moedim* (Gen 1:14), embedding covenant time into the fabric of creation. Paul declares that the festivals were "a shadow of what is to come; but the substance belongs to Christ" (Col 2:16-17). Every *moed* is a rehearsal pointing to its fulfillment — Passover to the cross, Firstfruits to the resurrection, Tabernacles to God's final dwelling with humanity.
