# בְּרִית — Berith

Covenant — the central structuring concept of biblical theology. A *berith* is a solemn, binding agreement between parties, typically ratified by oath, sacrifice, and stipulations. The Hebrew idiom is *karat berith* ("to cut a covenant"), reflecting the ancient ritual of cutting animals in two and passing between the pieces.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 9#v9|Gen 9:9]] — "I establish My covenant (*berith*) with you" (Noahic)
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 15#v18|Gen 15:18]] — "The LORD made a covenant (*berith*) with Abram" (Abrahamic)
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Exodus/Exodus 19#v5|Exod 19:5]] — "If you will keep My covenant (*berith*)" (Sinai/Mosaic)
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/2 Samuel/2 Samuel 7#v12|2 Sam 7:12]] — Davidic covenant promise
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 31#v31|Jer 31:31]] — "I will make a new covenant (*berith chadashah*)"

## Theological Significance

The covenants form the backbone of redemptive history: Noahic (preservation of creation), Abrahamic (promise of land, seed, blessing), Mosaic (law and national identity), Davidic (eternal kingship), and New (internalized law, universal forgiveness). Each *berith* advances God's plan while building on previous ones.

Jeremiah 31:31-34 announces the decisive shift: a "new covenant" in which Torah is written on hearts rather than tablets, and sins are forgiven comprehensively. Jesus identifies his blood with this new *berith* at the Last Supper (Luke 22:20), and the author of Hebrews builds his entire argument on the superiority of the new [[concepts/greek/G1242 - diatheke|diatheke]] over the old.
