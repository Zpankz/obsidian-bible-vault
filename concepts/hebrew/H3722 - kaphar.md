# כָּפַר — Kaphar

To atone, cover, or make atonement — the central verb of the sacrificial system. The root may mean "to cover" (related to *kopher*, "pitch/ransom") or "to wipe clean." *Kaphar* describes the ritual process by which sin's defilement is dealt with and the broken relationship between God and the sinner is restored.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Leviticus/Leviticus 16#v30|Lev 16:30]] — "On this day atonement (*kaphar*) shall be made for you to cleanse you"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Leviticus/Leviticus 17#v11|Lev 17:11]] — "The life of the flesh is in the blood… it is the blood that makes atonement (*kaphar*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 6#v14|Gen 6:14]] — "Cover (*kaphar*) it with pitch" (Noah's ark — same root)
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Exodus/Exodus 30#v10|Exod 30:10]] — "Aaron shall make atonement (*kaphar*) on its horns once a year"

## Theological Significance

Leviticus 17:11 is the interpretive key: God has *given* the blood on the altar to make atonement — atonement is God's provision, not a human bribe to appease deity. The blood, representing the life of the animal substitute, covers the sinner and cleanses the sanctuary. Yom Kippur (Day of Atonement) is the annual climax of this system, when the high priest enters the Most Holy Place.

The NT understands Christ's death through the lens of *kaphar*: Romans 3:25 presents Jesus as the *hilasterion* (mercy seat / propitiation), the place where God and sinful humanity meet through atoning blood. The Day of Atonement finds its ultimate fulfillment in Good Friday — Christ as both high priest and sacrifice entering the heavenly sanctuary once for all (Heb 9:11-14).
