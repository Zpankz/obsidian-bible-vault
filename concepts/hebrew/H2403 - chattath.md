# חַטָּאת — Chattath

Both "sin" and "sin offering" — the same Hebrew word covers the disease and its remedy. From the root *chata* ("to miss the mark, go astray"), *chattath* denotes deviation from God's standard. As a sacrificial term, it refers to the purification offering that cleanses the sinner and the sanctuary from contamination.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 4#v7|Gen 4:7]] — "Sin (*chattath*) is crouching at the door"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Leviticus/Leviticus 4#v3|Lev 4:3]] — "Let him offer… a sin offering (*chattath*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Psalms/Psalms 51#v2|Ps 51:2]] — "Cleanse me from my sin (*chattath*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Isaiah/Isaiah 53#v10|Isa 53:10]] — "If He would render Himself as a guilt offering"

## Theological Significance

The dual meaning of *chattath* — both sin and sin offering — is theologically profound. Sin is not merely a moral failure but a contamination that defiles the sanctuary where God dwells. The *chattath* offering absorbs this pollution, purifying the sacred space so God's presence can remain among the people.

Paul draws on this double meaning in 2 Corinthians 5:21: "He made Him who knew no sin to be sin (*hamartia*) on our behalf." Christ becomes the ultimate *chattath* — the sin-bearer who is simultaneously the sin offering, absorbing the contamination of human transgression to purify the entire creation as God's temple.
