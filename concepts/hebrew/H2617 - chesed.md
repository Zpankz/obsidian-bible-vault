# חֶסֶד — Chesed

The most theologically loaded word in the OT — variously translated "lovingkindness," "steadfast love," "mercy," or "loyal love." No single English word captures its range. *Chesed* is covenant faithfulness expressed in gracious action — love that persists despite the beloved's failure, loyalty that exceeds obligation.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Exodus/Exodus 34#v6|Exod 34:6]] — "Abounding in lovingkindness (*chesed*) and truth"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 136#v1|Ps 136:1]] — "His lovingkindness (*chesed*) is everlasting" (repeated 26 times)
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 23#v6|Ps 23:6]] — "Surely goodness and lovingkindness (*chesed*) will follow me"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Hosea/Hosea 6#v6|Hos 6:6]] — "I delight in loyalty (*chesed*) rather than sacrifice"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Micah/Micah 6#v8|Mic 6:8]] — "To love kindness (*chesed*)"

## Theological Significance

*Chesed* is God's covenant character in action. In Exodus 34:6-7 — the most-referenced creedal statement in the OT — God reveals Himself as "abounding in *chesed*." This is not merely that God is loving in general but that He is relentlessly faithful to His covenant promises, even when Israel is unfaithful. Psalm 136 drives this home by making *chesed* the refrain of all salvation history.

The prophet Hosea embodies *chesed* in his marriage to unfaithful Gomer: God's love pursues, redeems, and restores despite betrayal. Jesus quotes Hosea 6:6 ("I desire *chesed*, not sacrifice") twice in Matthew, identifying mercy as the heart of Torah. The NT concept of [[concepts/greek/G5485 - charis|grace (charis)]] is the closest heir to *chesed* — unmerited, covenant-rooted, inexhaustible divine favor.
