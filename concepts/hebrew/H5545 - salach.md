# סָלַח — Salach

To forgive, pardon — a verb used exclusively with God as its subject. No human being is ever the subject of *salach* in the OT; only God forgives in this sense. This is not merely an oversight but a theological claim: genuine forgiveness — the cancellation of guilt before the divine court — is God's exclusive prerogative.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Exodus/Exodus 34#v9|Exod 34:9]] — "Pardon (*salach*) our iniquity and our sin"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Numbers/Numbers 14#v19|Num 14:19]] — "Pardon (*salach*) the iniquity of this people"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/1 Kings/1 Kings 8#v30|1 Kgs 8:30]] — "Hear and forgive (*salach*)" (Solomon's temple prayer)
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 130#v4|Ps 130:4]] — "There is forgiveness (*selichah*) with You"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 31#v34|Jer 31:34]] — "I will forgive (*salach*) their iniquity"

## Theological Significance

The restriction of *salach* to God alone underscores that forgiveness is not a casual thing — it is a divine act that addresses guilt at its deepest level. Solomon's temple prayer (1 Kgs 8) repeatedly asks God to "hear and forgive," establishing the temple as the place where heaven touches earth and forgiveness flows.

The new covenant promise climaxes with *salach*: "I will forgive their iniquity and their sin I will remember no more" (Jer 31:34). When Jesus claims authority to forgive sins (Mark 2:7-10), the scribes rightly recognize the implication — He is exercising a prerogative that belongs to God alone. This is either blasphemy or revelation.
