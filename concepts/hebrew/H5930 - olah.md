# עֹלָה — Olah

Burnt offering — the most common sacrifice, in which the entire animal "goes up" (*alah*) in smoke on the altar. Unlike other offerings where portions are eaten, the *olah* is wholly consumed — nothing is retained by priest or worshiper. It expresses total consecration and surrender to God.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 8#v20|Gen 8:20]] — "Noah built an altar… and offered burnt offerings (*olot*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 22#v2|Gen 22:2]] — "Offer him there as a burnt offering (*olah*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Leviticus/Leviticus 1#v3|Lev 1:3]] — "If his offering is a burnt offering (*olah*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/1 Samuel/1 Samuel 15#v22|1 Sam 15:22]] — "To obey is better than sacrifice, than the fat of rams"

## Theological Significance

The *olah* teaches that approach to God requires total surrender — the animal represents the worshiper's entire self given over to God. The fire that consumes the sacrifice is not destruction but transformation — the offering ascends to God as a "soothing aroma" (Lev 1:9), a metaphor for divine acceptance.

Genesis 22 provides the most dramatic *olah* narrative: Abraham prepares to offer Isaac, and God provides a ram "in his place." This substitutionary logic — another dies so the beloved may live — runs through the entire sacrificial system and reaches its fulfillment in Christ, who "offered Himself without blemish to God" (Heb 9:14), the ultimate whole offering.
