# יָדַע — Yada

To know — not merely intellectual apprehension but experiential, relational, and intimate knowledge. Hebrew *yada* encompasses perception, skill, recognition, relationship, and sexual intimacy (Gen 4:1). When God "knows" someone, it means He has chosen them in covenant relationship; when humans "know" God, it means lived faithfulness.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 4#v1|Gen 4:1]] — "The man knew (*yada*) his wife Eve"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Exodus/Exodus 33#v13|Exod 33:13]] — "Let me know (*yada*) Your ways that I may know You"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 31#v34|Jer 31:34]] — "They will all know (*yada*) Me"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Amos/Amos 3#v2|Amos 3:2]] — "You only have I known (*yada*) among all the families of the earth"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 139#v1|Ps 139:1]] — "O LORD, You have searched me and known (*yada*) me"

## Theological Significance

When Amos 3:2 says God "knew" Israel alone, it does not mean God lacked information about other nations — it means He entered into covenant relationship exclusively with Israel. *Yada* is thus a covenant verb: to know is to commit, to choose, to be intimately involved. This relational epistemology stands in contrast to Greek detached contemplation.

The new covenant promise of Jeremiah 31:34 — "they will all know Me" — envisions universal, direct, intimate knowledge of God, no longer mediated exclusively through priests and prophets. Jesus claims this fulfillment: "This is eternal life, that they may know (*ginosko*) You" (John 17:3). Knowledge of God is not a curriculum but a relationship.
