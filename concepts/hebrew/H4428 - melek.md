# מֶלֶךְ — Melek

King, ruler — one of the most common nouns in the OT, applied to human kings, pagan rulers, and supremely to God Himself as the King of kings. YHWH's kingship is the theological baseline: human kings are accountable deputies, and the messianic hope centers on an ideal king who will reign with perfect justice.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Psalms/Psalms 47#v7|Ps 47:7]] — "God is the King (*melek*) of all the earth"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/1 Samuel/1 Samuel 8#v7|1 Sam 8:7]] — "They have rejected Me from being King (*melek*) over them"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Isaiah/Isaiah 6#v5|Isa 6:5]] — "My eyes have seen the King (*melek*), the LORD of hosts"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Psalms/Psalms 2#v6|Ps 2:6]] — "I have installed My King (*melek*) upon Zion"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Zechariah/Zechariah 9#v9|Zech 9:9]] — "Behold, your king (*melek*) is coming to you"

## Theological Significance

Israel's request for a human *melek* (1 Sam 8) is presented as a rejection of YHWH's direct kingship. Yet God accommodates the request and transforms it: the Davidic covenant promises an eternal throne (2 Sam 7), and the royal psalms envision a universal king who rules the nations in righteousness.

Zechariah 9:9 — the humble king riding on a donkey — redefines royal power: the messianic *melek* conquers not by force but by lowliness. Jesus' triumphal entry deliberately fulfills this vision, and the inscription on the cross ("King of the Jews") ironically declares what is theologically true. The NT culmination is Revelation 19:16: "King of kings and Lord of lords" — the crucified carpenter revealed as the cosmos's rightful sovereign.
