# רוּחַ — Ruach

Spirit, wind, breath — a single Hebrew word covering the Spirit of God, the human spirit, wind, and breath. This semantic unity is theologically intentional: the same force that moves over the primordial waters (Gen 1:2), animates human life (Gen 6:17), and empowers prophets (Num 11:25) is *ruach* — God's dynamic, life-giving, invisible power.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v2|Gen 1:2]] — "The Spirit (*ruach*) of God was moving over the surface of the waters"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 2#v7|Gen 2:7]] — God breathes *neshamah* (parallel to *ruach*) into man
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Isaiah/Isaiah 11#v2|Isa 11:2]] — "The Spirit (*ruach*) of the LORD will rest on Him"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Ezekiel/Ezekiel 37#v14|Ezek 37:14]] — "I will put My Spirit (*ruach*) within you and you will come to life"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Joel/Joel 2#v28|Joel 2:28]] — "I will pour out My Spirit (*ruach*) on all mankind"

## Theological Significance

*Ruach Elohim* in Genesis 1:2 is the first hint of the Spirit's distinct activity — hovering over chaos like a bird over a nest, preparing for the creative word. The Spirit is the agent of both physical life (Ps 104:30) and spiritual renewal (Ezek 37), making *ruach* the bridge between creation and new creation.

The prophetic hope centers on a universal outpouring of *ruach* (Joel 2:28) — no longer limited to select leaders but poured out "on all flesh." Peter identifies Pentecost as this fulfillment (Acts 2:16-17). The NT [[concepts/greek/G4151 - pneuma|pneuma]] directly continues the OT *ruach*: the same Spirit who hovered over the waters now indwells the church, making believers the living temple of God.
