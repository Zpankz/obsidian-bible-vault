# נֶפֶשׁ — Nephesh

Soul, life, self, person — the Hebrew word for the whole living being, not an immaterial ghost trapped in a body. From the root "to breathe," *nephesh* denotes the animated self with all its desires, needs, and vitality. Animals also have *nephesh* (Gen 1:20); what distinguishes human *nephesh* is the divine image.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 2#v7|Gen 2:7]] — "Man became a living being (*nephesh chayyah*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Deuteronomy/Deuteronomy 6#v5|Deut 6:5]] — "Love the LORD your God with all your… soul (*nephesh*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 42#v1|Ps 42:1]] — "As the deer pants… so my soul (*nephesh*) pants for You"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Leviticus/Leviticus 17#v11|Lev 17:11]] — "The life (*nephesh*) of the flesh is in the blood"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 23#v3|Ps 23:3]] — "He restores my soul (*nephesh*)"

## Theological Significance

*Nephesh* corrects the common misreading of "soul" as a disembodied spiritual substance. In Hebrew anthropology, the human does not *have* a nephesh — the human *is* a nephesh. Genesis 2:7 does not say God put a soul into a body; it says the dust, animated by God's breath, *became* a living nephesh. This holistic view underlies the biblical hope of bodily resurrection rather than mere spiritual escape.

The *nephesh* is also the seat of desire and longing — "my soul thirsts for God" (Ps 42:2). In the sacrificial system, "the nephesh is in the blood" (Lev 17:11), making blood sacred because it represents the life of the whole being. The NT echoes this when Jesus says He lays down His *psyche* for the sheep (John 10:11) — His whole self, not just a spiritual part.
