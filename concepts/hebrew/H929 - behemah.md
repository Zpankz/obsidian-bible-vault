# בְּהֵמָה — Behemah

A collective noun for beasts or large land animals, especially domesticated livestock. Distinguished from creeping things (*remes*) and wild animals (*chayyat ha'arets*). In Genesis 1, *behemah* belongs to the sixth day of creation alongside humanity, underscoring the kinship and distinction between humans and animals.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 1#v25|Gen 1:25]] — "God made the beasts (*behemah*) of the earth"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 1#v26|Gen 1:26]] — "Let them rule over… the cattle (*behemah*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 6#v7|Gen 6:7]] — "I will blot out man… and animals (*behemah*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Psalms/Psalms 49#v12|Ps 49:12]] — "Man… is like the beasts (*behemot*) that perish"

## Theological Significance

The *behemah* is both subject of human care and object lesson in human finitude. Humanity is given [[concepts/hebrew/H7287 - radah|dominion]] over *behemah* (Gen 1:26), yet the wisdom tradition notes that humans and animals share the same mortal breath (Eccl 3:19). The sacrificial system depends on *behemah* — the animal substitutes for the human, pointing to the costliness of atonement.

In Job 40:15, God invokes *Behemoth* (intensified form) as a creature of overwhelming power, humbling Job's pretensions to comprehend the created order. The beast thus serves both theology (human rule as divine delegation) and doxology (creation's grandeur exceeds human control).
