# אֶחָד — Echad

The Hebrew numeral "one," theologically central as the predicate of the Shema: "YHWH is one" (Deut 6:4). The word can denote simple numerical unity or compound unity (as in "one flesh," Gen 2:24), a distinction that has generated extensive theological discussion.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Deuteronomy/Deuteronomy 6#v4|Deut 6:4]] — "Hear, O Israel! The LORD is our God, the LORD is one (*echad*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 2#v24|Gen 2:24]] — "They shall become one (*echad*) flesh"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Zechariah/Zechariah 14#v9|Zech 14:9]] — "The LORD will be king… the LORD will be one (*echad*) and His name one"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 1#v5|Gen 1:5]] — "There was evening and morning, one (*echad*) day"

## Theological Significance

The Shema's declaration that YHWH is *echad* is the cornerstone of biblical monotheism. Whether *echad* here means "alone" (absolute unity) or "one" (unique, unified) has been debated since antiquity. In context, it functions as a covenant loyalty demand: YHWH alone is Israel's God.

The fact that *echad* can describe compound unity (two becoming "one flesh") has been noted by Christian theologians as compatible with — though not proving — trinitarian theology. The emphasis in the OT, however, is not on God's internal composition but on His exclusive claim: there is no other.
