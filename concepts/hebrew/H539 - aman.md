# אָמַן — Aman

The root behind "amen," "faith," and "truth" in Hebrew. In the Hiphil stem (*he'emin*) it means "to believe, trust, have faith." The root conveys firmness, reliability, and steadfastness — faith as leaning one's full weight on something solid.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 15#v6|Gen 15:6]] — "Then he believed (*he'emin*) in the LORD; and He reckoned it to him as righteousness"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Exodus/Exodus 14#v31|Exod 14:31]] — "The people feared the LORD, and they believed (*he'eminu*) in the LORD"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Isaiah/Isaiah 7#v9|Isa 7:9]] — "If you will not believe (*ta'aminu*), you surely shall not last (*te'amenu*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Habakkuk/Habakkuk 2#v4|Hab 2:4]] — "The righteous will live by his faith (*emunah*)"

## Theological Significance

Genesis 15:6 is the foundational text for the biblical doctrine of justification by faith — Abraham *believed* and it was *credited* as righteousness. Paul cites this verse as the cornerstone of his argument in Romans 4 and Galatians 3, building the NT doctrine of [[concepts/greek/G4102 - pistis|pistis]] (faith) on this Hebrew root.

Isaiah 7:9 contains a famous wordplay: *im lo ta'aminu ki lo te'amenu* — "if you do not believe (*aman*), you will not be established (*aman*)." Faith and stability share the same root. To trust God is to stand firm; to refuse trust is to collapse. This root gives us the liturgical "Amen" — an affirmation of truthfulness and trustworthiness.
