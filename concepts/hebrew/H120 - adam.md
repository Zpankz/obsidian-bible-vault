# אָדָם — Adam

The Hebrew word for humanity and the proper name of the first human being. Derived from אֲדָמָה (*adamah*, "ground/earth"), establishing the fundamental link between humanity and the soil from which it was formed. The word carries both individual ("the man") and collective ("humankind") senses.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v26|Gen 1:26]] — "Let Us make man (*adam*) in Our image"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 2#v7|Gen 2:7]] — "The LORD God formed man (*adam*) of dust from the ground (*adamah*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 5#v2|Gen 5:2]] — "He named them Man (*adam*) in the day they were created"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 8#v4|Ps 8:4]] — "What is man (*enosh*) that You take thought of him?"

## Theological Significance

The wordplay between *adam* and *adamah* is theologically intentional: humanity is earthbound, drawn from the dust, yet uniquely dignified as the [[concepts/hebrew/H6754 - tselem|image]] of God. This dual identity — dust and dignity — is the anthropological tension that runs through all of Scripture.

Paul picks up the Adam typology in Romans 5:12-21 and 1 Corinthians 15:45-49, contrasting the "first Adam" (earthly, mortal) with the "last Adam" (Christ, life-giving spirit). Adam is thus both a historical figure and a theological archetype representing all humanity in its solidarity with sin and death.
