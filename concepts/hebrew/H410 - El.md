# אֵל — El

The most basic Semitic word for deity, shared across ancient Near Eastern languages (Akkadian *ilu*, Ugaritic *il*). In the OT it refers to the true God, often in compound divine names: El Shaddai, El Elyon, El Olam. It emphasizes God's raw power and transcendence.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 14#v18|Gen 14:18]] — "priest of God Most High (*El Elyon*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 17#v1|Gen 17:1]] — "I am God Almighty (*El Shaddai*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 33#v20|Gen 33:20]] — "El, the God of Israel (*El Elohe Yisrael*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Isaiah/Isaiah 9#v6|Isa 9:6]] — "Mighty God (*El Gibbor*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 22#v1|Ps 22:1]] — "My God (*Eli*), my God, why have You forsaken me?"

## Theological Significance

While [[concepts/hebrew/H430 - Elohim|Elohim]] became the standard generic term for God, *El* retains a more archaic, primal force. Its compound forms reveal different facets of the divine character: *El Shaddai* (God Almighty) stresses sustaining power, *El Elyon* (God Most High) stresses cosmic supremacy, *El Olam* (Everlasting God) stresses eternal endurance.

Jesus' cry from the cross — "Eli, Eli, lama sabachthani?" (Matt 27:46) — uses this ancient word, quoting Psalm 22:1. The most primal name for God appears at the moment of deepest abandonment, connecting the crucifixion to Israel's oldest theological vocabulary.
