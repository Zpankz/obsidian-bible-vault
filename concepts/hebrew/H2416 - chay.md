# חַי — Chay

Living, alive, life — the adjective used to describe both God ("the living God") and His creatures. In the oath formula *chai YHWH* ("as the LORD lives"), it affirms that Israel's God is not a dead idol but a God of active, dynamic life. God is the source of all *chay*.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 2#v7|Gen 2:7]] — "Man became a living (*chay*) being"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Genesis/Genesis 3#v20|Gen 3:20]] — "Eve… the mother of all the living (*chay*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Deuteronomy/Deuteronomy 5#v26|Deut 5:26]] — "The voice of the living (*chay*) God"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Psalms/Psalms 42#v2|Ps 42:2]] — "My soul thirsts for God, for the living (*chay*) God"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Jeremiah/Jeremiah 10#v10|Jer 10:10]] — "The LORD is the true God; He is the living (*chay*) God"

## Theological Significance

Life in the OT is not an independent property of creation but a continuous gift from the living God. When God breathes into the dust, Adam becomes a *nephesh chayyah* ("living soul") — life is God's breath animating matter. This is why death is so grievous in the biblical worldview: it is the withdrawal of God's life-giving presence.

The title "living God" (*El chay*) is a polemic against the dead idols of the nations. Israel's God speaks, acts, sees, and hears — He is *alive*. This becomes the ground of resurrection hope: the God of the living will not abandon His faithful to death (Ps 16:10-11), a text Peter applies to Christ's resurrection in Acts 2:27.
