# גָּאַל — Gaal

To redeem, specifically by acting as a kinsman-redeemer (*goel*). The *goel* was the nearest male relative obligated to buy back family property, avenge blood, or marry a deceased relative's widow. The concept fuses legal obligation, family loyalty, and economic transaction into a single act of rescue.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Exodus/Exodus 6#v6|Exod 6:6]] — "I will redeem (*gaal*) you with an outstretched arm"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Ruth/Ruth 3#v13|Ruth 3:13]] — "If he will redeem (*gaal*) you, good; let him redeem"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Isaiah/Isaiah 43#v1|Isa 43:1]] — "I have redeemed (*gaal*) you; I have called you by name"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Job/Job 19#v25|Job 19:25]] — "I know that my Redeemer (*goel*) lives"
- [[bibles/NASB-IL/Isaiah/Isaiah 44#v6|Isa 44:6]] — "The LORD… the Redeemer (*goel*) of Israel"

## Theological Significance

When God is called Israel's *goel*, the metaphor is profoundly intimate — YHWH is not a distant deity but a close kinsman who claims His family out of bondage. The Exodus is the paradigmatic *gaal* event: God buys Israel back from slavery, not with money but with mighty acts. This establishes the pattern for all subsequent redemption theology.

The book of Ruth dramatizes *gaal* at the human level: Boaz as kinsman-redeemer prefigures Christ, who has the right (kinship through incarnation), the resources (his own life), and the willingness to redeem. The NT [[concepts/greek/G4982 - sozo|salvation]] vocabulary draws deeply on this well.
