# מָשִׁיחַ — Mashiach

Anointed one — from *mashach* ("to smear/anoint with oil"). Anointing set apart kings, priests, and occasionally prophets for their office. Over time, *mashiach* developed into a title for the expected future deliverer — the ultimate King from David's line who would restore Israel and inaugurate God's kingdom.

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/1 Samuel/1 Samuel 2#v10|1 Sam 2:10]] — "He will… exalt the horn of His anointed (*mashiach*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 2#v2|Ps 2:2]] — "Against the LORD and against His Anointed (*mashiach*)"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Isaiah/Isaiah 61#v1|Isa 61:1]] — "The LORD has anointed (*mashach*) me to bring good news"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Daniel/Daniel 9#v25|Dan 9:25]] — "Until Messiah (*mashiach*) the Prince"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Isaiah/Isaiah 45#v1|Isa 45:1]] — "To Cyrus, His anointed (*mashiach*)"

## Theological Significance

The *mashiach* concept fuses three anointed offices: king, priest, and prophet. Israel's hope crystallized around a coming figure who would unite all three — a royal priest who speaks God's word and rules with justice. The OT never fully specifies how these offices merge; that remains for the NT to reveal in Jesus.

The Greek translation [[concepts/greek/G5547 - Christos|Christos]] ("Christ") is a literal rendering of *mashiach*. When Peter confesses "You are the Christ" (Matt 16:16), he is declaring Jesus to be the long-awaited anointed one. But Jesus redefines the title through suffering — the Messiah must first be the Suffering Servant (Isa 53) before He is the reigning King, a combination no one expected.
