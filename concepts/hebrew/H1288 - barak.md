# בָּרַךְ — Barak

To bless — derived from *berek* ("knee"), suggesting that blessing is received in a posture of humility. *Barak* operates in three directions: God blesses humanity (conferring power and favor), humans bless God (praise and thanksgiving), and humans bless one another (patriarchal blessings, priestly benedictions).

## Key Passages

- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 1#v28|Gen 1:28]] — "God blessed (*barak*) them… Be fruitful and multiply"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 12#v2|Gen 12:2]] — "I will bless (*barak*) you, and make your name great"
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Numbers/Numbers 6#v24|Num 6:24]] — "The LORD bless (*barak*) you and keep you" (Aaronic benediction)
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Genesis/Genesis 27#v27|Gen 27:27]] — Isaac's blessing of Jacob
- [[bibles/NASB-Interlinear/Psalms/Psalms 103#v1|Ps 103:1]] — "Bless (*barak*) the LORD, O my soul"

## Theological Significance

Blessing in Genesis is the engine of creation's fruitfulness — God speaks *barak* over living creatures (1:22), over humanity (1:28), and over the Sabbath (2:3). The Abrahamic promise concentrates this cosmic blessing into a single family through which "all the families of the earth will be blessed" (Gen 12:3) — the missional trajectory of the entire Bible.

The priestly blessing of Numbers 6:24-26 is God's authorized channel of *barak* — the priest speaks the words, but "I will bless them" (6:27) makes clear that the power belongs to YHWH. In the NT, Jesus' ministry is the fulfillment of the Abrahamic blessing reaching the nations through the gospel (Gal 3:8-9, 14).
